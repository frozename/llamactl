Chapter 1. Loomings.

Call me Ishmael. Some years ago - never mind how long precisely - having little or no money in my purse, and nothing particular to interest me on shore, I thought I would sail about a little and see the watery part of the world. It is a way I have of driving off the spleen and regulating the circulation. Whenever I find myself growing grim about the mouth; whenever it is a damp, drizzly November in my soul; whenever I find myself involuntarily pausing before coffin warehouses, and bringing up the rear of every funeral I meet; and especially whenever my hypos get such an upper hand of me, that it requires a strong moral principle to prevent me from deliberately stepping into the street, and methodically knocking people's hats off - then, I account it high time to get to sea as soon as I can.

There now is your insular city of the Manhattoes, belted round by wharves as Indian isles by coral reefs, commerce surging in the streets, the Grand-Jury men of the archipelago, and the great world bustling around its wharves like a neighborhood at a fair. Consider the crowded water-front of the island, one vast pageant, where the river sees reflected all the life of the city. There are the ships of many nations, and the flags of every quarter of the globe, moving under a hundred different trades, and bound in all directions.

The town is full of little odds and ends of people, long rows of brick houses, and narrow lanes that run into the very heart of business. The evenings are bright with lamps, and the warehouses are dark, but for the little squares of light that mark the clerks at work among their ledgers. Dock laborers shoulder casks, captains consult charts, and apprentices carry messages between counting rooms while the tide comes in and goes out.

At the custom-house, the clerks mutter over receipts and manifests, and the harbor pilots compare notes about winds, shoals, and the habits of the shifting channels. A bell rings somewhere inland, and the sound travels over the harbor in a long thin line. Men in tarred hats point to the sky, and the gulls wheel over the masts in a white and screaming cloud.

On winter mornings the streets are bright and harsh, and every pane of glass seems to hold a pale reflection of the sea. In the counting rooms, ink freezes in the bottle unless the stove is fed, and the old desks smell of pine, salt, and lampblack. A clerk who has worked all night may step outside to find the air so keen that the breath rises like smoke.

The ferries thread their way between piers, carrying passengers from borough to borough, and each crossing gives a fresh view of the city, of warehouses and churches, of spires and chimneys, of ships' spars crowding the horizon. The water is always in motion, and with it the whole prospect shifts, a page painted over and over by the tide.

Sometimes the harbor is as still as a millpond, and sometimes it is roughened by a black wind from the north. Then the ropes creak, the rigging sings, and the gulls turn hard against the weather. Men pull their coats tight, and the lines of the piers look longer than before. The sea is never the same twice, and yet it keeps to its old argument with the shore.

Beyond the waterfront are the avenues, the elevated roads, and the long zigzags of traffic that climb toward the higher ground. By day carts rattle past the shops, and by evening the gaslights take hold of the corners. The city goes on speaking in freight, whistles, and footfalls, as if it were one large instrument keeping time to the harbor.

There are taverns with low ceilings and dark wood, where sailors tell stories of Cape Horn and the South Pacific, and where the maps on the wall are faded by smoke. There are boarding houses with steep stairs and narrow rooms, and from their windows one can sometimes see the masts standing like a winter forest against the sky.

The water-front, the avenues, the warehouses, and the ferries all keep the same restless rhythm, and the city itself seems to breathe with the tides. Yet for all its commerce and motion, there is a loneliness in the crowd, a sense that each person walks with a private weather, and that the harbor, for all its noise, keeps its own silence beneath the bells and the iron rails.
